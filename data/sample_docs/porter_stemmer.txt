The Porter stemming algorithm is a process for removing the commoner
morphological and inflexional endings from words in English. Its main
use is as part of a term normalization process that is usually done
when setting up information retrieval systems. The algorithm was
introduced by Martin Porter in 1980 and has since become a standard
component of search engines and text mining pipelines.

The stemmer works in five sequential steps. Each step inspects the
suffix of the word and rewrites it according to a small set of rules
that depend on the stem's measure: roughly the count of vowel-consonant
patterns in the leading portion of the word. Examples include reducing
"connection", "connections", "connective", and "connected" to a common
stem "connect".

Stemming improves recall in information retrieval by collapsing
morphological variants to a shared form, but it can hurt precision if
the rules are too aggressive. Lemmatization is a more linguistically
informed alternative that returns dictionary forms instead of
heuristic stems.
