Word embeddings are dense vector representations of words learned from
large text corpora. Unlike one-hot encodings, they place semantically
similar words close together in vector space, so simple geometric
operations capture meaningful linguistic relationships. The classic
analogy "king minus man plus woman approximately equals queen" became
the standard illustration of this property.

Popular methods include word2vec, GloVe, and fastText. Word2vec trains
a shallow neural network with either the skip-gram or continuous bag-of-
words objective. GloVe instead factorizes a global word co-occurrence
matrix. FastText extends word2vec with character-level information so
out-of-vocabulary words can still receive useful embeddings.

Modern systems usually replace static embeddings with contextualized
representations from transformer language models. Even so, static
embeddings remain useful as features for lightweight classifiers, in
low-resource settings, and as inputs to dense retrievers when latency
is critical.
